# Reel Format Library

This file is the single source of truth for reel format definitions. Each format defines: narrative structure, beat pattern, natural hook families, account stage fit, and goal type.

**What belongs here:** format scaffolds, beat patterns, narrative logic, purpose statements.  
**What does NOT belong here:** tag vocabulary, TTS rules, pipeline execution. Those live in `reel-template.md` and `docs/reel-pipeline.md`.

For format-to-hook affinity (format → H-family), see `agency/creative/hook-selection.md` — that file owns the affinity relationship, not this one.

# Format 1 — Market Signal (30s)

**Account stage:** cold, growing  
**Goal type:** exposure, authority  
**Hook families:** H1, H4, H6

### Purpose

Take one signal.

Explain what it suggests.

Best for:

- payment plans
- infrastructure
- pricing
- rental demand
- developer behavior
- absorption signals

### Structure

```text
[0–3s]   Hook
[3–12s]  What the signal means
[12–22s] Why investors care
[22–27s] Project connection
[27–30s] CTA
```

### Script Scaffold

```text
[VISUAL: Strong text hook]

[VO:]
"[Interesting signal]."

[PAUSE]

[VISUAL: Supporting visual]

[VO:]
"The interesting part isn't [surface thing].

It's what this might suggest."

[VISUAL: Explanation graphic]

[VO:]
"[Explain the signal in plain language]."

[VISUAL: Project connection]

[VO:]
"In [Project Name],
that's what makes this interesting."

[VISUAL: CTA]

[VO:]
"[Tier 2 CTA]"
```

### Hebrew Example — Sierra

```text

[PAUSE]

[VO:]

מספרת יותר מהברושור."

[VO:]

שווה לשאול למה."

שהייתי בודקת ב-Sierra."

[VO:]
```

# Format 2 — Investment Thesis (45s)

**Account stage:** cold, growing  
**Goal type:** authority, conversion  
**Hook families:** H7, H8, H9

### Purpose

Build one clear investment thesis.

NOT:
Full project pitch.

Focus on:

"What is the actual investment logic here?"

Best for:

- appreciation plays
- macro trends
- pricing inefficiencies
- area transformation
- tourism or infrastructure demand

### Structure

```text
[0–4s]   Hook
[4–15s]  Bigger idea
[15–28s] Why this project matters
[28–38s] Reality check → reframe
[38–45s] CTA
```

> The [28–38s] beat is 10 seconds — enough for both the risk and its closure. It must contain the risk statement AND a reframe question or return to thesis within the same segment. CTA follows immediately; there is no separate reframe beat. Do not let the last VO line of [28–38s] be the raw risk.

### Script Scaffold

```text
[VISUAL: Strong hook]

[VO:]
"The interesting question here isn't [obvious thing]."

[VISUAL: Market context]

[VO:]
"[Explain bigger market dynamic]."

[VISUAL: Project connection]

[VO:]
"[Project Name]
gets interesting if you believe [thesis]."

[VISUAL: Reality check → reframe]

[VO:]
"The honest risk: [real risk].
[Reframe question or return to thesis — this is the last VO line before CTA.]"

[VISUAL: CTA]

[VO:]
"[Tier 2 CTA]"
```

### Hebrew Example — Costa Mare

```text
[VO:]
"כולם מסתכלים על Wynn.

פחות אנשים מסתכלים
על מה שקורה סביבו."

[VO:]
"בנדל״ן,
המחיר הרבה פעמים זז
לפני שהתשתית נפתחת."

[VO:]
"זה מה שהופך את Costa Mare
למעניין."

[VO:]
"לא בלי סיכון.
RAK עדיין שוק צעיר יותר מדובאי.
השאלה היא אם הקצב של Wynn מצדיק את הפרמיום."

[VO:]
"כתבו לי RAK
ואשלח את הניתוח המלא."
```

# Format 3 — Myth Bust (30s)

**Account stage:** cold  
**Goal type:** exposure, authority, trust  
**Hook families:** H7, H8

### Purpose

Challenge a bad assumption.

Best for:

- beginner education
- trust building
- authority

Avoid generic myths.

Prefer investor myths.

### Myth Bank

- "Luxury projects are safer"
- "Cheap entry price = better investment"
- "High yield means good investment"
- "Beachfront always wins"
- "Payment plans make projects affordable"
- "Branded residence = good investment"
- "New area = early opportunity"
- "8% תשואה זה אומר עסקה טובה" (a high yield number alone doesn't mean the deal is good)
- "לוקיישן טוב זה תמיד השקעה טובה" (a good location doesn't guarantee a good investment)

### Structure

```text
[0–3s]   Myth
[3–18s]  Reality
[18–26s] Investor implication
[26–30s] CTA
```

### Script Scaffold

```text
[VISUAL: Myth statement]

[VO:]
"'[Myth]'"

[PAUSE]

[VISUAL: Contradiction]

[VO:]
"Sometimes true.

Often not."

[VISUAL: Explanation]

[VO:]
"[Explain what actually matters]."

[VISUAL: Investor implication]

[VO:]
"The better question is:
[actionable investor question]."

[VISUAL: CTA]

[VO:]
"[Tier 2 CTA]"
```

### Hebrew Example

```text
[VO:]
"'יוקרה אומרת השקעה בטוחה יותר.'"

[PAUSE]

[VO:]
"לפעמים.

אבל יוקרה
לא מגינה עליך
מלשלם יותר מדי."

[VO:]
"השאלה המעניינת:
מה כבר מתומחר?"

[VO:]
"כתבו לי DUBAI
ואשלח את הניתוח."
```

# Format 4 — Area Thesis (45s)

**Account stage:** cold, growing  
**Goal type:** exposure, authority  
**Hook families:** H1, H4, H8

### Purpose

Help investors understand an area.

NOT:
Sell an area.

Focus on:

- demand drivers
- infrastructure
- pricing
- timing
- investor behavior

### Structure

```text
[0–4s]   Hook
[4–18s]  What most people miss
[18–32s] Why now — include Reality Check here if relevant
[32–40s] Thesis return / Investor question
[40–45s] CTA
```

> If this reel introduces a risk or supply concern, place it in the [18–32s] segment, not [32–40s]. The [32–40s] beat is always a thesis return, investor question, or forward frame. Never a risk statement.

### Script Scaffold

```text
[VISUAL: Area name]

[VO:]
"[Area Name].

Here's what most investors miss."

[VISUAL: Area context]

[VO:]
"[Demand driver / pricing story / infrastructure]."

[VISUAL: Timing signal — include risk here if relevant]

[VO:]
"[Why this matters now.]
[If risk applies: state the risk here, not below.]"

[VISUAL: Thesis return]

[VO:]
"[Project Name] only matters if you believe [thesis].
[Surface the investor question that connects thesis to decision.]"

[VISUAL: CTA]

[VO:]
"[Tier 2 CTA]"
```

### Hebrew Example — JVC

```text

[VO:]
"הוא מעניין
כי הביקוש לשכירות

איך מסתכלים
```

# Format 6 — Personal Experience (20–40s)

**Account stage:** cold  
**Goal type:** exposure, authority  
**Hook families:** H1, H8

### Purpose

Tell a real investment story in first person. Outcome disclosed at the start — the story explains the reasoning behind it, not what happened.

The investor is not pitching. The investor is processing out loud.

Best for:

- personal investment decisions the user has made
- "here's what I was thinking when I bought X"
- market experiences with surprising results
- timing stories (bought before / after a catalyst)

NOT:
- fabricated examples
- generic market commentary dressed as personal story
- outcome without genuine reasoning

### Structure

```text
[0–4s]   Outcome hook — disclose a real result or decision upfront
[4–14s]  What I actually bought / decided and why
[14–24s] The logic I was running at the time
[24–32s] What the honest uncertainty was
[32–38s] Where it stands now / the question that remains
[38–40s] CTA
```

Duration adapts to the story. A tight story can land in 20s. A case with more context can run to 40s.

### Script Scaffold

```text
[VISUAL: atmospheric — place or moment]

[VO:]
"[Outcome or decision stated as fact. No setup.]"

[VISUAL: context]

[VO:]
"[What I bought / did. Specific, not general.]"

[VISUAL: reasoning beat]

[VO:]
"[The logic I was running. Not 'here's what the market said' — here's what I was thinking.]"

[VISUAL: honest uncertainty]

[VO:]
"[The risk I accepted. What I didn't know. What I couldn't know.]"

[VISUAL: where it stands / the open question]

[VO:]
"[What's still open. The question worth asking from here.]"

[VISUAL: CTA]

[VO:]
"[Tier 2 CTA]"
```

### Hebrew Notes

- VO must sound like the person is thinking out loud, not narrating a presentation
- First person, past tense for decisions made, present tense for the open question
- Avoid: "אז הבנתי ש..." — too structured; prefer "וזה מה שגרם לי לחשוב ש..."
- The hook line should disclose a real number, real decision, or surprising result — not a teaser

### Hebrew Example Scaffold

```text
[VO:]
"[תוצאה אמיתית. עובדה. בלי הקדמה.]"

[VO:]
"[מה קניתי / עשיתי. ספציפי.]
[הלוגיקה שרצה אצלי בזמן.]"

[VO:]
"[הסיכון שקיבלתי. מה לא ידעתי.]"

[VO:]
"[מה עדיין פתוח. השאלה שנשארת.]"

[VO:]
"[CTA Tier 2]"
```

# Format 7 — Contrarian / Reframe (20–35s)

**Account stage:** cold, growing  
**Goal type:** authority, engagement  
**Hook families:** H7, H8

### Purpose

Challenge a widely-held belief with calm analytical reasoning. The goal is to make the viewer feel smarter for watching — not to shock them.

The tone is not combative. It's "here's a more interesting way to look at this."

Best for:

- conventional wisdom that is mostly right but misses something
- popular market takes that are missing a dimension
- framing traps ("everyone says X, but the interesting question is Y")
- positioning the brand as a thinker, not a promoter

NOT:
- contrarian for contrarian's sake
- "everyone is wrong and I'm right"
- myths (use Format 3 for explicit myth-busting)

### Structure

```text
[0–4s]   State the conventional belief — with calm, not mockery
[4–14s]  Introduce the crack — where the belief partially breaks down
[14–24s] The better frame — not just "you're wrong" but "here's what's more useful"
[24–30s] The investor question — what this frame suggests for decision-making
[30–35s] CTA
```

### Script Scaffold

```text
[VISUAL: conventional belief or market framing]

[VO:]
"[State the conventional belief as if it's reasonable.]
[One beat of acknowledgment that it's not entirely wrong.]"

[VISUAL: the crack]

[VO:]
"[Where it breaks. The dimension people are missing.]"

[VISUAL: better frame]

[VO:]
"[The reframe. What's actually worth paying attention to.]"

[VISUAL: investor question]

[VO:]
"[The investor question this frame leads to. Not an answer — an invitation to think.]"

[VISUAL: CTA]

[VO:]
"[Tier 2 CTA]"
```

### Hebrew Notes

- The hook must state the belief without mocking it — credibility comes from engaging the belief seriously
- The crack must be specific, not generic ("the market is complex")
- Closing question should feel earned, not rhetorical

# Format 8 — Comparison (25–40s)

**Account stage:** growing, established  
**Goal type:** authority, engagement  
**Hook families:** H1, H4

### Purpose

Compare two options, two areas, two approaches. The viewer leaves with a better framework for thinking, not just a verdict.

Best for:

- two competing markets (RAK vs. Dubai)
- two investment approaches (yield play vs. appreciation play)
- two developer strategies
- before/after a market event

The comparison must have a real dimension that most people overlook. Otherwise it's just a list.

NOT:
- obvious comparisons where one option is clearly better
- comparisons that are really just a pitch for one option dressed as balance

### Structure

```text
[0–4s]   Frame the comparison — name both sides
[4–14s]  The obvious dimension — what most people compare
[14–24s] The hidden dimension — what actually changes the decision
[24–32s] The verdict or the investor's real question
[32–38s] Honest caveat or contextual constraint
[38–40s] CTA
```

The verdict does not have to declare a winner. "It depends on X" is a stronger closer than a forced choice.

### Script Scaffold

```text
[VISUAL: side A]

[VO:]
"[Side A] vs. [Side B].
[Most people compare them on X.]"

[VISUAL: side B]

[VO:]
"[Where B wins on the obvious dimension. Be fair.]"

[VISUAL: hidden dimension]

[VO:]
"[The dimension that most people skip.
The one that actually changes which is right for which investor.]"

[VISUAL: verdict or investor question]

[VO:]
"[The reframe. A verdict if one is defensible, or the question the investor should actually be asking.]"

[VISUAL: caveat]

[VO:]
"[The honest constraint. Which investor profile this works for and which it doesn't.]"

[VISUAL: CTA]

[VO:]
"[Tier 2 CTA]"
```

### Hebrew Notes

- Avoid "A vs. B" as the literal hook — frame the choice as a question, not a competition
- The hidden dimension beat is the most important — without it, this is just a list
- If the verdict is genuinely unclear, say so explicitly and make that the insight

# Format 9 — Mistake / Lesson (20–35s)

**Account stage:** cold  
**Goal type:** exposure, trust  
**Hook families:** H7, H6

### Purpose

Confession structure: what most investors get wrong (or what the speaker got wrong), and the insight that came from it.

The hook is the mistake, not the lesson. Lead with the error.

Best for:

- common investor misconceptions that cause real harm
- pricing errors (paying the wrong number for the wrong reason)
- timing errors (buying or waiting based on a flawed mental model)
- due diligence gaps (what people don't check that they should)

NOT:
- mistakes that are obvious in hindsight but weren't wrong at the time
- lessons that are generic advice ("do your research")
- self-promotion disguised as humility

### Structure

```text
[0–4s]   The mistake — stated bluntly, from inside the person who made it
[4–14s]  Why it seemed right — the logic that led to it
[14–24s] What actually happened / the signal that was missed
[24–30s] The real lesson — one sentence, not a list
[30–35s] CTA
```

The lesson must be specific and actionable. "I should have checked X" beats "I should have done more research."

### Script Scaffold

```text
[VISUAL: the mistake moment or context]

[VO:]
"[The mistake. First person if it's personal, second person if it's a common pattern.]"

[VISUAL: the logic that led to it]

[VO:]
"[Why it seemed right. Show the reasoning, not the foolishness. Most investors would have done the same.]"

[VISUAL: the signal that was missed]

[VO:]
"[The thing that, in hindsight, was already there.]"

[VISUAL: the lesson]

[VO:]
"[One specific, actionable lesson. Not a warning — a new question to ask.]"

[VISUAL: CTA]

[VO:]
"[Tier 2 CTA]"
```

### Hebrew Notes

- The mistake must be stated with confidence, not shame — it reads as intelligence, not weakness
- "הרבה משקיעים עושים את זה" is stronger than "אני טעיתי" for cold audiences who don't know the speaker yet
- The lesson beat must end before the CTA — do not let the CTA carry the lesson

# Format 10 — Opportunity (20–35s)

**Account stage:** cold  
**Goal type:** exposure, engagement, conversion  
**Hook families:** H1, H4

### Purpose

Surface something most people are not paying attention to yet. Not hype — a reasoned case for why a specific market signal or situation is worth investigating now.

The hook creates urgency through information, not through pressure.

Best for:

- early-stage markets before a catalyst event
- pricing inefficiencies that are temporary
- supply/demand dynamics that most investors are reading incorrectly
- timing windows tied to external events (regulatory change, infrastructure opening, developer incentives)

NOT:
- vague urgency ("now is the time to invest")
- opportunities that are already widely known
- anything that requires ignoring the downside case

### Structure

```text
[0–4s]   Attention hook — name the thing most people are missing
[4–14s]  What's actually happening — the signal, the data, the context
[14–22s] Why the timing matters — the window and why it exists
[22–28s] The honest risk — why others haven't acted on this
[28–33s] The investor question — what to verify before acting
[33–35s] CTA
```

The honest risk beat is required. An opportunity reel without a risk beat is just hype.

### Script Scaffold

```text
[VISUAL: the overlooked signal]

[VO:]
"[Most people are focused on X. The more interesting question is Y.]"

[VISUAL: context / data]

[VO:]
"[What's actually happening. One or two specific, data-grounded observations.]"

[VISUAL: timing]

[VO:]
"[Why this window exists. What created it and when it closes.]"

[VISUAL: honest risk]

[VO:]
"[Why this isn't priced in yet — and what could go wrong.]"

[VISUAL: investor question]

[VO:]
"[What an investor should verify before acting on this.]"

[VISUAL: CTA]

[VO:]
"[Tier 2 CTA]"
```

### Hebrew Notes

- The hook must be specific — "שוק שאנשים לא מכירים" is too vague; "60 ק"מ מדובאי ולא מדברים על זה" is specific
- The honest risk beat is not optional — an Opportunity reel without a risk beat reads as marketing, not intelligence

# Format 11 — Case Study (35–50s)

**Account stage:** growing, established  
**Goal type:** authority, conversion  
**Hook families:** H8, H9

### Purpose

One real example, beginning to end. Proof through story, not through data.

The case study works when the story is more persuasive than the numbers. The numbers appear, but they're proof points for the narrative, not the narrative itself.

Best for:

- a real investment decision with a visible outcome
- a market event with a traceable effect on pricing or demand
- a developer or project with a track record worth examining
- before-and-after stories where the thesis played out (or didn't)

NOT:
- hypothetical case studies
- stories where the outcome is still unknown (use Format 6 — Personal Experience instead)
- outcomes that cherry-pick favorable data without acknowledging the conditions that created it

### Structure

```text
[0–4s]   The outcome — what happened, stated as a result
[4–14s]  The setup — who, what, when, where; the context before the decision
[14–25s] The bet — what the investor was actually betting on (not what they said, what they were betting)
[25–35s] How it played out — what happened, what the signals were along the way
[35–42s] The lesson or open question — what this case tells us about the market, the thesis, or the decision framework
[42–48s] Honest constraint — when does this case NOT generalize
[48–50s] CTA
```

### Script Scaffold

```text
[VISUAL: the outcome — place, number, result]

[VO:]
"[State the outcome. Specific numbers if they're available and defensible.]"

[VISUAL: the setup]

[VO:]
"[The context before the decision. What the market looked like at the time.]"

[VISUAL: the bet]

[VO:]
"[The actual investment logic. What had to be true for this to work.]"

[VISUAL: how it played out]

[VO:]
"[What happened. The signals that confirmed or complicated the thesis along the way.]"

[VISUAL: lesson or open question]

[VO:]
"[What this case tells us. A lesson that is specific to this case, not a generic investing rule.]"

[VISUAL: honest constraint]

[VO:]
"[When this doesn't generalize. The conditions that made this work that may not exist elsewhere.]"

[VISUAL: CTA]

[VO:]
"[Tier 2 CTA]"
```

### Hebrew Notes

- The outcome hook must use a real number or a real named result — "מישהו הרוויח" is too vague
- The "bet" beat is the most important — it should name the actual thesis, not dress it as luck or instinct
- The honest constraint beat is what separates a case study from a success story; without it, the reel reads as marketing
